If you only ever learn one idea from finance, make it this one. Compound interest is the engine behind almost every long-term financial outcome — retirement accounts, credit card debt, mortgages, and the fortunes of patient investors.

## The one-sentence version

Compound interest means **you earn interest on your interest** — so your money grows on top of growth, not just on what you originally put in.

That small difference is the whole game.

## Simple vs. compound: a quick example

Imagine you invest **$1,000** at **10% per year**.

With *simple* interest, you'd earn a flat $100 every year. After 30 years you'd have $4,000.

With *compound* interest, each year's gains start earning too:

- Year 1: $1,000 → $1,100
- Year 2: $1,100 → $1,210
- Year 3: $1,210 → $1,331

After 30 years, that $1,000 becomes about **$17,449** — more than four times the simple-interest result, from the exact same starting point.

> The money you didn't spend keeps working. That's the part beginners underestimate.

## Why time matters more than amount

The most powerful variable in compounding isn't how much you invest — it's **how long** you let it run.

Someone who invests $200/month from age 25 to 35 and then *stops* often ends up with more at retirement than someone who invests the same amount from 35 to 65. The early starter gave their money decades of extra compounding.

The lesson: **starting early beats starting big.**

## The dark side: debt compounds too

The same math that builds wealth can bury you. Credit card balances often compound at 20%+ per year. Left unpaid, a small balance snowballs the same way investments do — just in the wrong direction.

## What to actually do with this

1. Start investing *something*, even a small amount, as early as possible.
2. Reinvest your returns instead of cashing them out.
3. Pay off high-interest debt aggressively — you're "earning" that interest rate by avoiding it.
4. Be patient. Compounding looks boring for years, then becomes dramatic.

Master this single idea and most other financial decisions get easier to reason about.
