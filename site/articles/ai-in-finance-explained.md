Artificial intelligence in finance gets talked about in two unhelpful extremes: breathless hype or vague fear. The reality is more grounded — and more interesting. AI is already woven into the financial system in specific, measurable ways. Here are the ones that matter.

## 1. Fraud detection

This is AI's oldest and most successful job in finance. Every time you swipe a card, a model scores the transaction in milliseconds: Is this purchase consistent with your history? Is the location plausible? Is the merchant risky?

These systems learn patterns no human could track across billions of transactions, flagging the suspicious ones for a freeze or a verification text.

## 2. Credit scoring and lending

Lenders increasingly use machine learning to estimate how likely a borrower is to repay. Instead of a handful of rules, models weigh hundreds of signals at once.

> This power comes with responsibility: if the training data reflects past bias, the model can quietly repeat it. Good lenders audit for exactly this.

## 3. Algorithmic and quantitative trading

A large share of market activity is now driven by algorithms. Some simply execute big orders without moving the price; others hunt for fleeting statistical patterns. Machine learning helps these systems adapt as market conditions shift.

Speed matters here — but so does discipline. The famous failures usually come from models trusted blindly in conditions they were never trained on.

## 4. Customer service and operations

The chatbots and virtual assistants in banking apps are the visible layer. Behind the scenes, AI also:

- reads and classifies documents (think loan paperwork),
- automates compliance checks,
- and summarizes mountains of regulatory text.

This is unglamorous and enormously valuable — it removes slow, repetitive human work.

## 5. Risk and forecasting

Banks model risk constantly: market risk, credit risk, liquidity risk. AI helps run more scenarios, faster, and spot fragile spots earlier than traditional models.

## The honest limitations

- **Explainability:** regulators often demand to know *why* a model denied a loan. Black-box answers aren't good enough.
- **Data quality:** a model is only as good as the history it learned from.
- **Overconfidence:** markets change; yesterday's pattern is not a guarantee.

## The takeaway

AI in finance isn't a robot picking stocks for you. It's thousands of quiet, specialized systems making the existing machinery faster, cheaper, and — when built carefully — fairer. The winners won't be the firms with the flashiest models, but the ones who deploy them with judgment.
